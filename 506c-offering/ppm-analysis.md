#### Key Movements (Sep–Dec 2025)

- **Cash burned $2.6M in 4 months** — monthly burn of ~$649K. This is significantly lower than the $2.2M/month full-year average, suggesting either expense reduction in Q4 or timing of cash receipts.

- **Accumulated deficit deepened by $14.9M** — the Sep–Dec net loss was $14.93M, implying a monthly loss rate of ~$3.73M.

### 4.2 Revenue Discrepancy — Critical Finding

| Period | Revenue | Source |
|---|---:|---|
| 8 months ended Aug 31, 2025 | 15,341,860 | PPM |
| 12 months ended Dec 31, 2025 | 12,942,362 | Financial Statements |
| **Implied Sep–Dec 2025** | **(2,399,498)** | **Calculated** |

**The PPM reports $15.34M in revenue for 8 months (Jan–Aug), but the full-year financial statements show only $12.94M for 12 months.** This means the implied Sep–Dec revenue is negative $(2.4M).

This is not mathematically possible for a subscription SaaS business unless:

1. **Revenue was restated or reclassified** between the PPM preparation (Nov 2025) and the year-end financial statement preparation (Jan 2026)
2. **Different recognition methodology** — the PPM may have used a different revenue figure (e.g., cash collected or bookings) vs. ASC 606 recognized revenue in the financial statements
3. **Reversal of previously recognized revenue** — subscription cancellations, refunds, or adjustments in Q4 could reverse earlier-recognized amounts
4. **Accounting adjustment at year-end** — the PPM interim figures may not have reflected adjustments that were applied when preparing full-year statements

**This discrepancy requires clarification.** In a $120M offering, a $2.4M revenue gap between the PPM and auditable financials is material. Investors relying on the PPM's 8-month revenue figure to extrapolate annual run-rate would arrive at ~$23M annualized — nearly double the actual $12.94M FY result.

### 7.1 What the PPM Says

**The extracted PPM does not include a specific use-of-proceeds breakdown.** This is a notable omission for a $120M offering. Standard 506(c) PPMs typically include a table or section detailing planned allocation (e.g., R&D, sales & marketing, working capital, general corporate purposes).

| **Sales & marketing scale-up** | Cut 63% in 2025 to $1.2M; needs rebuilding for growth | $5–15M |

### 7.3 Runway Implications

| Scenario | Monthly Burn | Runway from $120M |
|---|---:|---:|
| Current burn rate ($2.24M/month ops) | $2,244,697 | ~53 months (4.4 years) |

### 9.2 Comparative Context

| Company / Benchmark | Revenue Multiple | Revenue Growth | Gross Margin | Profitability |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| **TA Fintech (PPM)** | **88.7x** | **34.6%** | **71.8%** | **Net loss = 274% of revenue** |
| Median public SaaS (2025) | 6–12x | 15–25% | 70–80% | Varies; many profitable |

At 88.7x revenue, the valuation is **3.5–6x higher** than comparable high-growth SaaS companies and **6–15x higher** than the broader SaaS median. This premium would typically require one or more of:
- Revenue growth exceeding 100% YoY
- Clear path to profitability within 2–3 years
- Dominant market position or proprietary technology moat
- Recurring revenue with >90% net retention

The company's 34.6% revenue growth, $35.5M annual loss, zero proprietary IP registration, and uneven revenue history (decline in 2024, potential discrepancy in 2025) do not obviously support multiples at this level.

## 10. Tax Position & NOLs

### 10.1 Net Operating Loss Carryforwards

| **Delaware state NOL** | Not quantified | Begins expiring in 2043 |
| **Deferred tax asset (Dec 2024)** | $15,994,016 | Fully offset by valuation allowance |

### 10.2 NOL Value Analysis

| Scenario | Taxable Income Needed | At 21% Fed Rate | 80% Limitation Applied |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Use all ~$89M NOL | $111.6M taxable income | $18.8M tax savings | Would offset 80% of income until exhausted |
| 5 years at $10M profit | $50M taxable income | $10.5M tax savings | $40M NOL used, ~$49M remaining |

- **Full valuation allowance** — the company has applied a 100% valuation allowance against its deferred tax asset, meaning management has concluded it is not "more likely than not" that the NOLs will be utilized. This is a conservative but telling position — it signals that management itself does not project sufficient near-term profitability to realize the tax benefit.
- **Section 382 risk** — if the 506(c) offering (or the Stone–Cruz control transfer) constitutes an "ownership change" under IRC Section 382, the annual utilization of pre-change NOLs could be significantly limited. The PPM does not address Section 382 implications.
- **C-corp structure** — as a C corporation, losses cannot be passed through to shareholders. The NOLs benefit only the corporation if/when it becomes profitable.

### 10.3 Implications for Investors

- NOLs have no direct value to 506(c) investors in a C-corp structure — they cannot use the losses on their personal returns.
- The NOLs are only valuable if the company reaches sustained profitability, which management's own valuation allowance suggests is uncertain.
- A Section 382 ownership change could cap annual NOL usage at a fraction of the accumulated amount, potentially rendering much of the $89M theoretical benefit inaccessible.
