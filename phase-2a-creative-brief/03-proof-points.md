# Key Proof Points — Ranked by Conversion Impact
## Phase 2a Creative Brief | The Signal VSL

---

## Ranking Methodology

Each proof point is scored on three dimensions:
- **Believability (B):** How credible is this to a skeptical viewer? (1–10)
- **Emotional impact (E):** How much does this move the viewer toward purchase? (1–10)
- **Uniqueness (U):** Can any competitor make this same claim? (1–10)

**Conversion Impact Score = B × E × U** (max 1,000)

## Proof Sequencing Strategy

The proof points should escalate in a specific sequence designed to dismantle skepticism layer by layer:

### Layer 1: "This exists." (Minutes 8–12)
- Proof Points: #4 (5 live portfolios), #1 (488 trades), specific winning trade examples
- Goal: Establish that the system is real, not hypothetical.

### Layer 2: "This is honest." (Minutes 12–14)
- Proof Points: #2 (showing losses), #10 (not a backtest)
- Goal: Build trust by doing what no competitor does — radical transparency.

### Layer 3: "This works." (Minutes 14–18)
- Proof Points: #5 (vs. S&P benchmark), #12 (navigating specific market events)
- Goal: Demonstrate aggregate performance including losses.

### Layer 4: "This is serious." (Minutes 18–22)
- Proof Points: #3 (504 securities), #9 (institutional infrastructure), #8 (managed accounts)
- Goal: Establish institutional credibility. This isn't a hobbyist project.

### Layer 5: "I want this." (Minutes 22–25)
- Proof Points: #6 (re-entry rate = active management), #7 (weekly rebalancing), present-day portfolio preview
- Goal: Bridge from historical proof to present-tense access. Create desire for the daily experience.

## Proof Point Presentation Rules

1. **Every proof point must be visually demonstrated.** Don't just say "488 trades." Show the trade log scrolling on screen. Don't just say "beats the S&P." Show the chart.

2. **Specificity over generality.** "MRVL scored 94/100 on March 12, entered at $87.40" beats "our AI picks winning stocks." Always use specific tickers, dates, and prices.

3. **Losses before wins.** Counterintuitive but essential. When you show losses first, everything that follows is more believable. The losses are the credibility tax that makes the wins tax-free.

4. **Let data breathe.** After a major proof point, pause for 3–5 seconds. Let the viewer process. Don't immediately rush to the next claim. Silence after data = confidence.

5. **Never make forward-looking claims.** Always past tense: "The AI did X." Never: "The AI will do X." This is both a compliance requirement and a credibility strategy. If the past performance is strong enough, the viewer projects it forward on their own — which is more persuasive than any claim we could make.

6. **Repeat the core number.** "488 trades" should appear at least 3 times in the VSL. Repetition creates anchoring. By the third mention, it's a fact they accept, not a claim they evaluate.

## Proof Points NOT to Use

These are tempting but counterproductive:

| Don't Use | Why |
|-----------|-----|
| "X% returns" as a headline | Attracts greed-motivated subscribers who churn fast. Let them calculate returns themselves from the data shown. |
| Testimonials from current subscribers | We don't have them yet, and fabricated ones destroy trust. The track record IS the testimonial. |
| "Our AI is better than [Specific Competitor]" | Direct competitor attacks invite legal scrutiny and look petty. Let the comparison chart speak for itself. |
| Projected future performance | FTC/SEC violation. Show what the AI DID. Never what it WILL do. |
| Complexity / technical jargon | "Neural network architecture" or "transformer-based NLP" — the viewer doesn't care. They care about the scoreboard. |
