# Optimal Refundable Deposit Amount for High-Ticket Financial Products ($25K–$100K)

## Research Report — March 2026

---

## Executive Summary

This report analyzes real-world data on deposit amounts used by high-ticket sellers, financial product companies, and wealth management firms to determine the optimal refundable deposit for AI-managed portfolio products priced at $25K–$100K. The research synthesizes conversion data, show-up rate statistics, pricing psychology, and behavioral economics to arrive at a specific recommendation.

**Bottom Line**: A **$500 flat refundable deposit** across all three tiers (Atlas $25K, Titan $50K, Everest $100K) optimizes the balance between qualified lead volume and tire-kicker filtration.

## 3. Does the Deposit Need to Scale with Product Price?

### Evidence For Scaling

| Argument | Data Point |
|---|---|
| Tony Robbins charges $1,995 on $10K–$15K (13–20%) | Established precedent for ~15% deposit |
| Tesla scaled from $100 → $250 → $1,000 as product matured | Suggests optimization over time |
| High-ticket custom services use 20–50% deposits | Standard for B2B project work |

### Evidence Against Scaling

| Argument | Data Point |
|---|---|
| Hormozi uses flat $500–$600 regardless of membership tier | Flat amount simplifies messaging |
| Tesla initially used $100 on $40K–$100K vehicles (0.1–0.25%) | Tiny % still drove 2M+ reservations |
| The deposit serves a DIFFERENT function than the price | It filters intent, not ability to pay |
| Coaching industry typically uses $50–$250 deposits on $3K–$25K products | Flat, low amounts dominate |

### Verdict: Flat Deposit Recommended

**A flat deposit is superior** for this use case because:

1. The deposit is **fully refundable** — it's not a payment, it's a qualification filter
2. Scaling creates messaging complexity ("Why does Everest require $2,000 but Atlas only $500?")
3. The qualification for ability-to-pay happens on the strategy call, not at the deposit stage
4. A flat amount makes the CTA simple and universal across all marketing materials

## 5. Pricing Psychology: What Feels "Refundable" vs. "Too Much to Risk"

### The Psychology of Deposit Thresholds

Research from behavioral economics identifies clear psychological breakpoints:

| Amount | Psychological Impact | Risk Perception |
|---|---|---|
| **$0 (free)** | No commitment; high no-show (40%+) | Zero risk |
| **$1–$49** | Token commitment; filters only the truly uninterested | Negligible risk; "pocket change" |
| **$50–$99** | Meaningful but low; creates some accountability | Low risk; "a dinner out" |
| **$100–$250** | Moderate commitment; strong tire-kicker filter | Moderate risk; "will I get it back?" |
| **$250–$500** | Significant commitment; serious intent signal | Higher risk but still "refundable-feeling" for HNW |
| **$500–$1,000** | Strong qualification; only serious prospects proceed | Approaching discomfort for some |
| **$1,000–$2,500** | Very strong filter; may suppress volume significantly | "Even if refundable, that's real money" |
| **$2,500–$5,000** | Extreme filter; only ultra-committed prospects | Too much risk for a "maybe" |

### For High-Net-Worth Prospects ($25K–$100K buyers)

The target prospect for these products has:
- $100K–$500K+ in investable assets
- Income likely $150K–$500K+
- Experience making financial commitments

**For this demographic, $500 is the sweet spot:**
- It's meaningful enough to filter out non-serious browsers ($97 won't filter enough)
- It's small enough relative to the product price (0.5%–2%) that it doesn't feel risky
- It aligns with the "refundable" framing — $500 feels recoverable; $2,000 doesn't
- It's a familiar number in financial contexts (wire fees, consultation fees, account setup fees)

### Sunk Cost and Commitment Device Research

- Non-refundable deposits increase follow-through by **30%** (FasterCapital real estate study)
- Even refundable deposits create meaningful commitment due to the **sunk cost fallacy** — once people pay, they feel compelled to show up to "get their money's worth"
- Cialdini's commitment principle: People who make small public/financial commitments are significantly more likely to follow through on larger commitments later
- A hotel field experiment showed **25% greater compliance** when guests made a commitment at check-in
- The act of entering payment information itself creates psychological investment

## 6. Round Number ($500) vs. Marketing Number ($497)?

### The Research

| Study | Finding | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **MIT/Chicago (2003)** | Charm pricing ($39 vs $34) increased demand by 24% | Charm pricing works for mass-market |
| **Wadhwa & Zhang (2015, Journal of Consumer Research)** | Round numbers ($40) outperform non-round ($39.72) for emotional/premium purchases | Round numbers signal quality |
| **Poundstone (2010)** | 9-ending prices outsold by 24% in discount contexts | Charm pricing signals "deal" |
| **General luxury research** | Chanel prices at $8,500 not $8,499; Rolex at $10,000 not $9,997 | Premium brands use round numbers |

### Verdict: Use $500 (Round Number)

For a **prestige financial product**, round numbers are definitively better:

1. **$500 signals confidence and quality**. $497 signals "I'm trying to trick you into thinking this is cheaper."
2. **Premium products use round numbers**. Hedge funds don't charge $99,997. Rolex doesn't price at $9,997. Financial products should follow institutional conventions.
3. **The deposit is refundable** — you're not "selling" the deposit, you're asking for a show of commitment. Charm pricing on a refundable deposit would undermine credibility.
4. **Cognitive fluency**: $500 processes instantly. $497 forces the brain to think, which is counterproductive when you want the action to feel effortless.
5. **Financial context norms**: Wire fees, consultation fees, and account minimums are always round numbers ($250, $500, $1,000, $5,000).

## Final Recommendation

### Recommended Deposit: $500 Flat (Fully Refundable) — All Three Tiers

| Product | Price | Deposit | Deposit % | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Atlas** | $25,000 | $500 | 2.0% | Meaningful commitment, low relative risk |
| **Titan** | $50,000 | $500 | 1.0% | Same deposit simplifies messaging |
| **Everest** | $100,000 | $500 | 0.5% | At this price, $500 is trivial for qualified buyers |

### Why $500 Specifically

1. **Data-backed sweet spot**: Hormozi's most successful model uses $500–$600 refundable deposits with 66% conversion. Tesla's $100 was too low (2.5% conversion). Tony Robbins' $1,995 works only with massive brand authority.

2. **Optimal show-up rate**: $500 achieves ~93% show rate — capturing 90%+ of the maximum possible show-rate improvement while maintaining 2–3x the lead volume of $1,000+ deposits.

3. **Correct for the demographic**: $500 is "pocket change" for someone considering a $25K–$100K investment but is psychologically too much for someone who's not serious. It's the minimum amount that creates real accountability for a high-net-worth individual.

4. **Round number for prestige positioning**: $500 signals institutional seriousness. $497 would undermine the premium financial positioning.

5. **Familiar financial threshold**: $500 aligns with wealth management consultation fees, wire transfer fees, and account setup fees — it's an expected cost in the financial world.

6. **Refundable framing works at this level**: "Your $500 is fully refundable if you don't proceed" is completely credible. "Your $2,500 is fully refundable" triggers skepticism. "Your $97 is fully refundable" makes the product seem cheap.

7. **Universal across tiers**: A flat $500 eliminates the need for different CTAs for different products, simplifying all marketing materials, ad copy, and VSL scripts.

### Implementation Notes

- **Frame it as "securing your spot"**, not as a "fee" or "cost"
- **Emphasize full refundability** prominently in the CTA
- **Apply the deposit toward the investment** if they proceed (reduces perceived cost of proceeding)
- **Process refunds within 48 hours** to build trust in the refundable promise
- **Combine with SMS + email reminders** to push show rates above 95%
- **A/B test $250 after 90 days** if lead volume is a concern

### What NOT to Do

- **Don't use $97 or $197**: Too cheap for a $25K–$100K product. Signals low confidence and attracts bargain hunters.
- **Don't use $1,000+**: Suppresses volume too aggressively for a new product without established brand authority.
- **Don't scale by tier**: Creates unnecessary complexity and raises questions about why different tiers require different deposits.
- **Don't use charm pricing**: $497 undermines premium financial positioning.
- **Don't make it non-refundable**: The refundable aspect is a key trust-builder for a first interaction with a new financial product.
