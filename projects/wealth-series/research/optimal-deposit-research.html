Executive Summary

This report analyzes real-world data on deposit amounts used by high-ticket sellers, financial product companies, and wealth management firms to determine the optimal refundable deposit for AI-managed portfolio products priced at $25K–$100K.

The research synthesizes conversion data, show-up rate statistics, pricing psychology, and behavioral economics to arrive at a specific recommendation.

3 Should the Deposit Scale with Product Price?

Evidence FOR Scaling

  • Robbins: $1,995 on $10K–$15K (13–20%)
  • Tesla scaled $100 → $250 → $1,000
  • B2B custom services: 20–50% deposits

Evidence AGAINST Scaling

  • Hormozi: flat $500–$600 regardless of tier
  • Tesla $100 on $40K–$100K drove 2M reservations
  • Deposit filters intent, not ability to pay
  • Scaling creates messaging complexity

Verdict: Flat Deposit

A flat deposit is superior for this use case because:

  1. The deposit is fully refundable — it’s a qualification filter, not a payment
  2. Scaling creates messaging complexity (“Why does Everest require $2,000 but Atlas only $500?”)
  3. Ability-to-pay qualification happens on the strategy call, not at deposit stage
  4. A flat amount simplifies all marketing materials, ad copy, and VSL scripts

6 Round Number ($500) vs. Marketing Number ($497)?

Study Finding Implication
MIT/Chicago (2003) Charm pricing increased demand by 24% Works for mass-market products
Wadhwa & Zhang (2015) Round numbers ($40) outperform non-round ($39.72) for premium purchases Round = quality signal
Luxury brand practice Chanel: $8,500 not $8,499. Rolex: $10,000 not $9,997 Premium = round numbers
Cognitive fluency Round numbers process instantly; non-round triggers analytical thinking $500 = effortless action

Verdict: $500 (Round Number)

  1. $500 signals confidence. $497 signals “I’m trying to trick you.”
  2. Hedge funds don’t charge $99,997. Financial products follow institutional conventions.
  3. The deposit is refundable — charm pricing on a refundable deposit undermines credibility.
  4. Financial context norms: Wire fees, consultation fees, and account minimums are always round numbers.

Why $500 Specifically

  1. Data-backed sweet spot. Hormozi’s most successful model uses $500–$600 refundable deposits with 66% conversion. Tesla’s $100 was too low (2.5% conversion). Robbins’ $1,995 requires massive brand authority.
  2. Optimal show-up rate. $500 achieves ~93% show rate — capturing 90%+ of maximum show-rate improvement while maintaining 2–3x the lead volume of $1,000+ deposits.
  3. Correct for the demographic. $500 is “pocket change” for someone considering $25K–$100K but psychologically too much for someone not serious.
  4. Round number for prestige. $500 signals institutional seriousness. $497 undermines premium positioning.
  5. Familiar financial threshold. Aligns with wealth management consultation fees, wire fees, and account setup fees.
  6. Refundable framing works. “$500 fully refundable” is credible. “$2,500 fully refundable” triggers skepticism. “$97 fully refundable” makes the product seem cheap.
  7. Universal across tiers. Flat $500 simplifies all marketing materials and CTA copy.

Do

  • Frame as “securing your spot”
  • Emphasize full refundability
  • Apply deposit toward investment
  • Refund within 48 hours
  • Combine with SMS + email reminders
  • A/B test $250 after 90 days

Don’t

  • Use $97 or $197 (too cheap for $25K+ product)
  • Use $1,000+ (suppresses volume for new brand)
  • Scale deposit by tier (adds complexity)
  • Use charm pricing ($497 undermines prestige)
  • Make it non-refundable (kills trust)
