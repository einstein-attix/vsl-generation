How Einstein + Claude Code Built
"What Investors Really Want"

A behind-the-scenes breakdown of the AI-managed research process that produced a 1,477-line, investor-grade competitive intelligence report in under 3 hours
Prepared for Carlos Cruz, CEO — February 22, 2026

The Big Picture

The "What Investors Really Want" V2 report was produced using a two-agent adversarial research process — a methodology where one AI creates the initial research, then a second AI instance brutally critiques it, and finally the critique is used to produce a dramatically improved final version.

This isn't just "AI writing a report." It's a structured intellectual workflow with distinct roles, quality gates, and iterative refinement — managed by Einstein (the strategic architect) and executed by Claude Code (the production engine running in tmux).

The core principle: A single AI pass produces B- work. But when you pit one AI's output against another AI's criticism, then synthesize the result, you get A-tier work. The adversarial loop is what creates quality. This is the same principle behind peer review in academia, red-teaming in security, and dialectical reasoning in philosophy.

Why This Matters for TradeAlgo

This process is repeatable and scalable. Any research question — competitor analysis, market sizing, product strategy, SEO audit, ad copy testing — can be run through this same pipeline. What took a team of analysts weeks can now be done in hours, with higher rigor, broader coverage, and built-in quality control.

The Two Agents & Their Roles

🧪 Einstein — The Architect & Manager

Einstein's role is strategic direction, quality control, and management. Einstein never writes production content. Instead, Einstein:

  • Designed the research framework — decided what questions to answer, what structure the report should follow, what depth was needed
  • Created the initial draft — wrote the V1 report as a comprehensive starting point (the "thesis" to be challenged)
  • Managed the tmux session — started Claude Code, sent it tasks, unblocked permission prompts, monitored progress
  • Defined the critique parameters — gave Claude Code 8 specific dimensions to critique (not just "review this")
  • Quality-gated the output — reviewed the critique, then crafted the V2 production brief based on what the critique revealed
  • Handled delivery — sent the final report to Carlos with context on what was included

⚡ Claude Code (tmux) — The Production Engine

Claude Code's role is execution, critique, and heavy-lifting. Claude Code:

  • Read and analyzed the V1 report — ingested the full 42KB HTML document
  • Produced the brutal critique — a 287-line, section-by-section teardown with specific grades, missing elements, and prioritized improvements
  • Built V2 from scratch — wrote 1,477 lines of production HTML incorporating all critique feedback, new sections, expanded analysis, citations, and three-scenario revenue modeling
  • Self-managed complexity — when the output exceeded token limits, autonomously pivoted to a chunked writing approach (4 sequential sections)

The division of labor is critical. Einstein thinks about what and why. Claude Code executes the how. Neither could produce this alone — Einstein lacks the production capacity for a 1,477-line document, and Claude Code lacks the strategic context to know what questions matter for TradeAlgo's business. Together, they're more than the sum of their parts.

The 7-Step Process

1

Stimulus & Framing

Einstein

Carlos asked: "What do investors really want? What makes our competitors successful? Is it loss prevention? Greed? Trends? Trade ideas? Portfolio management? Entertainment? Other factors?"

Einstein's first move was to expand the question. Carlos listed 6 possible drivers. Einstein hypothesized there were more, and that the real answer required understanding the psychology behind the behavior, not just the behavior itself. This reframing — from "what do they want" to "what emotional needs drive their spending" — became the central thesis of the report.

2

Initial Research Draft (V1)

Einstein

Einstein wrote the complete V1 report — 42KB of HTML covering 12 psychological drivers, 5 investor segments, competitor-by-competitor analysis, churn triggers, unmet needs, and a revenue framework. This draft was intentionally comprehensive but imperfect.

Key design decision: The V1 was written to be a strong starting point, not a final product. Einstein knew it would be critiqued and improved. This is counterintuitive — most people try to make their first draft perfect. But in the adversarial process, a "good enough" V1 gives the critic more to work with than a polished V1 that hides its weaknesses.

3

Spin Up the Critic (Claude Code in tmux)

Einstein

Einstein launched a dedicated Claude Code tmux session specifically for this research task. Why tmux and not a background process? Because tmux allows:

  • Persistent sessions that survive disconnects
  • Real-time monitoring of progress via tmux capture-pane
  • Interactive unblocking (permission prompts, confirmations)
  • Multiple sessions running in parallel (the ai-sales-agent session continued running simultaneously)
4

The Adversarial Critique

Claude Code

This is where the magic happens. Einstein didn't just say "review this" — Einstein gave Claude Code 8 specific critique dimensions:

  1. Missing psychological drivers (anchoring, endowment effect, herding)?
  2. Are investor segments too simplistic?
  3. Are we being too generous to competitors?
  4. Are "unmet needs" actually being addressed by startups?
  5. Is the revenue math realistic?
  6. What data sources should we cite?
  7. Are there entire missing categories?
  8. Is the emotional framing too manipulative?

The specificity of the critique prompt is what produced a specific, actionable critique rather than vague feedback. The quality of the critique is directly proportional to the quality of the critique prompt.

5

Critique Delivered: The Brutal Teardown

Claude Code

Claude Code produced a 287-line critique that graded the V1 report across 9 dimensions. The verdict: C+/B-. Key findings:

6

V2 Production Brief

Einstein

After reviewing the critique, Einstein crafted a detailed production brief for Claude Code. This brief didn't just say "fix the problems" — it specified exactly 12 improvements to incorporate, in what order, with what structure. Einstein also made the editorial decision to incorporate all Priority 1 and Priority 2 improvements but skip Priority 3 (which required primary research that wasn't available).

This is the management layer. The critic identified 15 issues. The manager decided which 12 to address now, which 3 to defer, and how to prioritize. Without this editorial judgment, the V2 would either try to do too much or miss the most important improvements.

7

V2 Production (Chunked Writing)

Claude Code

Claude Code read both the V1 and the critique, then began writing V2. The document was so large (137KB, 1,477 lines) that it exceeded the 32K output token limit on the first attempt. Claude Code autonomously adapted — it pivoted to writing the document in 4 sequential chunks:

  • Chunk 1 (493 lines): Cover, thesis, 12 drivers, behavioral economics framework, expanded segments
  • Chunk 2 (→ 778 lines): Competitor complaints, what makes them loved, unmet needs with competitive context
  • Chunk 3 (→ 1,034 lines): Additional needs categories, ethical guardrails, regulatory risk, TradeAlgo framework
  • Chunk 4 (→ 1,477 lines): 3-scenario revenue math, distribution strategy, competitive moat, citations

Einstein monitored each chunk, approved edits, and unblocked prompts as needed. Total production time for V2: approximately 90 minutes.

Why The Adversarial Process Produces Superior Output

1. It Defeats Confirmation Bias

When a single author writes a report, they unconsciously build the argument they believe in. They emphasize supporting evidence and minimize contradictions. The V1 report did exactly this — it was a persuasive pitch for TradeAlgo's opportunity, not a rigorous analysis of the market.

The critic doesn't share the author's biases. Claude Code came in fresh and immediately spotted: "This reads like it was written to convince TradeAlgo's team that this is a great opportunity rather than to rigorously analyze the market." That single observation transformed the V2 from a pitch deck into a strategic document.

2. It Catches Blind Spots Through Orthogonal Thinking

Einstein's V1 was implicitly US-centric, male-coded, and equity-focused. These weren't deliberate choices — they were invisible assumptions. The critique surfaced them: "Where are international investors? Female investors? Crypto-native investors? Income-focused investors?" These blind spots would have persisted through any number of self-revisions because the original author couldn't see what they weren't thinking about.

3. It Forces Intellectual Honesty

The V1 claimed "7 unmet market needs" that TradeAlgo could fill. The critique responded: "Several of these gaps are actively being addressed by Betterment, Wealthfront, Danelfin, Toggle AI, and others." This forced the V2 to be honest about competitive threats rather than pretending they don't exist. The result is a more credible, more useful document.

4. It Elevates Rigor

The V1 had zero citations. Specific claims like "loss aversion is 2x stronger than the desire for gains" had no source. The critique called every unsourced claim: "Where do these numbers come from? These would not survive 30 seconds of investor scrutiny." The V2 includes 20+ numbered citations and labels estimates explicitly.

5. It Discovers Missing Dimensions

The critique didn't just improve what existed — it identified entire categories the V1 missed: tax optimization, income generation, behavioral coaching, ESG investing, estate planning, regulatory risk, distribution strategy, competitive response analysis, and ethical guardrails. The V1 had 6 sections. The V2 has 15.

The meta-insight: The first AI draft is never the best possible output — it's the best possible starting point for improvement. The value isn't in the V1 itself; it's in creating something concrete enough to critique. You can't critique a blank page. But once you have a B- draft, you can systematically upgrade every dimension to produce an A.

What Went Wrong (Honest Assessment)

The process wasn't flawless. Here's what we'd improve next time:

🔴 The Sub-Agent Stall (30 minutes lost)

When V2 exceeded the 32K token limit, Claude Code attempted to spawn a sub-agent to write the file. This sub-agent ran for 30+ minutes without producing any output. Einstein should have intervened sooner — the 10-minute mark was the right time to redirect, not the 30-minute mark. Lesson: Set explicit time-boxes for each approach and fail fast.

🟡 Permission Prompt Friction

Claude Code's safety prompts ("Do you want to proceed?") required manual approval multiple times. While important for safety, this added latency. The "allow all edits during this session" option should have been selected from the start. Lesson: Pre-configure trust levels for known-safe operations.

🟡 Pasted Text Submission

The initial critique prompt was pasted into tmux but didn't auto-submit — it required a manual Enter key. This caused a brief delay while Einstein diagnosed why the session wasn't processing. Lesson: Always verify command submission after tmux paste operations.

🟢 V1 Could Have Been Sharper

If the V1 had included even basic citations and a more honest assessment of competitors, the critique would have had fewer "easy" issues to flag and could have focused more on deeper strategic gaps. Lesson: Invest more in V1 quality to get higher-value critique feedback.

How To Replicate This For Any Research Task

This process works for any deep research or analysis task. Here's the reusable framework:

The Adversarial Research Protocol

1

Frame the Question Deeply

Don't accept the surface question. Carlos asked "what makes competitors successful?" Einstein expanded this to "what psychological needs drive investors to pay, stay, and love financial newsletters?" The depth of the framing determines the depth of the output.

2

Produce a Comprehensive V1

Write the best first draft you can. Don't aim for perfection — aim for completeness. Cover every dimension you can think of. This gives the critic maximum surface area to work with.

3

Define Specific Critique Dimensions

Don't just say "review this." Specify 5-10 exact dimensions to evaluate. Examples: "Are the assumptions realistic?" "What's missing?" "What would an investor say?" "What would a competitor say?" "Where are the unsourced claims?" The more specific the critique prompt, the more actionable the feedback.

4

Send to a Fresh AI Instance for Critique

Use a separate session (tmux, sub-agent, or separate conversation) so the critic doesn't share context or biases with the author. The critic should read only the V1 document and the critique prompt — nothing else.

5

Managerial Triage

Review the critique and decide which improvements to incorporate. Not all critique is equally valuable — some points are critical, some are nice-to-have, some are wrong. The human or managing agent makes editorial decisions about priority.

6

Produce V2 with Explicit Improvement List

Don't say "incorporate the feedback." List every specific improvement to make. This ensures nothing is lost in translation and gives the production agent a clear checklist.

7

Verify and Deliver

Spot-check the V2 against the critique. Did every Priority 1 issue get addressed? Are the citations real? Does the revenue math have scenarios? Then deliver with context so the recipient knows what they're reading.

This entire protocol can be applied to: Competitor analysis, market sizing, product strategy, content strategy, SEO audits, ad copy testing, technical architecture reviews, business plans, investor presentations, customer research, pricing analysis — any task where rigor and completeness matter more than speed alone.

The secret is the adversarial loop — create, critique, synthesize — managed by an agent that knows what questions matter for the business. The V1 was generic. The critique made it honest. The V2 is a genuine strategic asset. The process is what transforms AI output from "impressive demo" to "I'd bet the business on this analysis."
